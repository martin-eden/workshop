Notes on CSV format and implementation


At first glance CSV looks like TSV.

Imagine you are in 1960s and have paper printout of TSV data.
You want to parse it yourself.. Fuck, you can't distinguish TAB
from SPC! It's all yellowish paper!

So yeah, let's just use "," instead of ASCII 9 ('\t')!

Problem is that "," is common character and so your fields can't
contain ",".


Two solutions arouse.

First idea (1960s?)

Let's use more weird (but printable) character! Like "~"! Or "|"!..
Or "$"..

Yeah this may work but you need to specify that fields separator
character somewhere. And format doesn't have place for this.

( My original implementation from 2017 supported another field
  separators. This one fixed to ",". We grew stubborn. )


Second idea (1970s?)

Quotes!

  > data_value,"field ,,, with commas!"

Fine. Quoting also frees newline \n:

  > data_value,"Wheee!!!
  >
  > It's multi-line value!"

Yeah, but how you can have " in data then? And it'll probably break
existing flavor of non-quoted CSV:

  > data_value,No problem "dudes"!

Let's just treat "" as " if we are after first "!

  > data_value,"No problem ""dudes""!"

Yeah it's fucking ugly but works.

( I would design stream format with overriding instructions for
  field and record separators. But I'm in 2026. )

So if field starts with " then it's quoted field and
that starting " won't come to it's contents.

Parsing double quotes is also some challenge. Because it looks that
if you are inside quotes and received " ("No problem ") you still can't
decide whether quotes are closed or not.

Also ending of quote does not means ending of field:

  > data_value,"This" is "valid"!

( I think I handled these cases nicely in this implementation.
  You can have fun too if you write your own CSV parser without
  looking at code. )

So be aware, CSV (unlike TSV) actually is ad-hoc format with local
idiosyncrasies and dirty data.

-- Martin, 2026-07-14
